> **Rule for this chapter**
>
> We are **NOT** reading Solidity yet.

# ❓ First Question

Is `UniswapV2Arb1` part of the Uniswap V2 protocol?

## Our Initial Thought

At first glance, seeing the name:

```solidity
contract UniswapV2Arb1
```

it almost feels like another Uniswap contract.

Maybe another protocol contract like:

- Factory
- Pair
- Router
- Library

---

## 💡 Realization

No.

This contract is **NOT** part of Uniswap V2.

Uniswap V2 already provides:

- Factory
- Pair
- Router
- Library

Those contracts together form the DEX.

This contract is **our own smart contract**.

It simply **uses** the Uniswap protocol.

Think of it like this.

Uniswap says:

> "I provide liquidity."

SushiSwap says:

> "I also provide liquidity."

Our contract says:

> "Thanks.
>
> I'll use both of you to make money."

Huge difference.

---

# What Does This Contract Actually Do?

Another realization we had.

This contract:

❌ Does not provide liquidity.

❌ Does not own reserves.

❌ Does not determine prices.

❌ Does not mint LP tokens.

❌ Is not an AMM.

Instead,

its only purpose is:

```text
Buy Cheap

↓

Sell Expensive

↓

Profit
```

That's literally its entire job.

---

# Bob Example

Suppose Bob notices:

| Exchange | Price |
|----------|-------|
| Uniswap | 3000 DAI / WETH |
| SushiSwap | 3100 DAI / WETH |

Bob immediately realizes:

"I can buy on Uniswap and sell on SushiSwap."

That's arbitrage.

Our contract simply automates what Bob already knows.

---

# ❓ Question

Does this contract FIND arbitrage?

Initially this is easy to misunderstand.

Maybe the contract checks prices.

Maybe it searches exchanges.

Maybe it decides which DEX to use.

---

## 💡 Realization

No.

The contract never asks:

- Which Router?
- Which token?
- Which DEX?
- Which amount?
- Which direction?

Someone else already figured that out.

Usually:

- Bob
- A bot
- A backend
- An MEV searcher

They discover the opportunity.

Then they simply tell the contract:

> Execute this.

So another huge realization was:

**This contract executes arbitrage.**

It does **NOT** discover arbitrage.

---

# ❓ Question

How many arbitrage strategies does this contract implement?

Initially we got confused.

Because we saw two public functions.

```solidity
swap(...)

flashSwap(...)
```

Our first thought was:

Maybe these are two completely different arbitrage strategies.

---

## 💡 Realization

No.

There is only ONE arbitrage strategy.

```text
Buy Cheap

↓

Sell Expensive

↓

Profit
```

That strategy never changes.

What changes is only:

**Where the starting money comes from.**

---

# The Two Funding Methods

This was probably the biggest realization.

The contract asks only one question.

> Where is the starting capital coming from?

There are only two answers.

---

## Method 1

Bob already has money.

```text
Bob's Wallet

↓

swap()

↓

Execute Arbitrage
```

---

## Method 2

Bob doesn't have money.

```text
Borrow Money

↓

flashSwap()

↓

Execute Arbitrage

↓

Repay Loan
```

Same arbitrage.

Different funding source.

This is a very important distinction.

The contract does **NOT** implement two arbitrage strategies.

It implements:

- One arbitrage strategy.
- Two funding methods.

---

# House Analogy (One of Our Realizations)

There is still only ONE goal.

```text
Buy House
```

But there are two ways to pay.

Method 1


Only the funding changes.

# Capital Arbitrage (Normal Arbitrage)

Suppose Bob already owns:

```text
3000 DAI
```

He sees:

| Exchange | Price |
|----------|-------|
| Uniswap | 3000 DAI |
| SushiSwap | 3100 DAI |

Bob calls:

```solidity
swap(params)
```

The execution flow becomes:

```text
Bob
 │
 │ Calls swap()
 ▼
Arb1
 │
 │ Pull Bob's DAI
 ▼
_swap()
 │
 ▼
Router0
 │
 ▼
Buy WETH
 │
 ▼
Router1
 │
 ▼
Sell WETH
 │
 ▼
Receive More DAI
 │
 ▼
Return Principal + Profit To Bob
```

Notice something.

There is:

❌ No borrowing.

❌ No Flash Swap.

❌ No callback.

❌ No Pair.swap(data).

Bob already owns the money.

The contract simply automates the execution.

---

# One Of Our Questions

Could Bob perform this arbitrage manually?

The answer surprised us.

Yes.

Bob could simply perform:

Transaction 1

```text
DAI

↓

WETH

(On Uniswap)
```

Transaction 2

```text
WETH

↓

DAI

(On SushiSwap)
```

The arbitrage would still work.

---

# Then Why Use A Smart Contract?

This was another realization.

The smart contract isn't required because arbitrage needs Solidity.

It exists because it gives us:

- One transaction.
- Atomic execution.
- Automatic profit protection.
- Automation.

---

## One Transaction

Instead of:

```text
Swap

↓

Wait

↓

Swap
```

Bob simply performs:

```text
swap()

↓

Done
```

---

## Atomicity

Suppose Bob manually buys WETH.

Before he sells it,

someone changes the price.

Now Bob is stuck holding WETH.

With the contract,

both swaps happen inside one transaction.

If something goes wrong,

everything reverts.

Nothing happened.

---

## Profit Protection

The contract checks whether enough profit was made.

If not,

the entire transaction reverts.

Bob never executes a bad arbitrage.

---

## Automation

Humans may perform one arbitrage.

Bots may perform thousands.

Instead of manually interacting with two exchanges,

they simply call:

```text
swap()

↓

Done
```

The contract becomes a reusable arbitrage engine.

---

# Final Understanding Before Reading Solidity

At this point,

we haven't read a single line of Solidity.

But we already understand something much more important.

This contract is **not another Uniswap contract.**

It is an arbitrage executor.

It performs one arbitrage strategy:

```text
Buy Cheap

↓

Sell Expensive

↓

Profit
```

using one of two funding methods:

1. Bob's own money (`swap()`).

