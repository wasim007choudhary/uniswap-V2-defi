# Flash Swap Fee — Why Do We Calculate It Ourselves?

One question naturally came up while studying `uniswapV2Call()`.

Inside `_swap()` we already perform two normal swaps.

Those swaps already pay the normal Uniswap trading fee.

So why do we calculate another fee here?

```solidity
uint256 fee = ((params.amountIn * 3) / 997) + 1;
uint256 amountToRepay = params.amountIn + fee;
```

Wouldn't the fee already have been deducted?

---

# The Confusion

At first it seems like we are paying twice.

```text
Swap Fee

+

Flash Swap Fee
```

But these are **not the same payment**, even though they both originate from Uniswap's fee model.

---

# Two Different Things

## 1. Trading Fee

Whenever we perform:

```text
DAI

↓

WETH
```

or

```text
WETH

↓

DAI
```

the Pair automatically charges the normal Uniswap trading fee.

We never calculate this ourselves.

It is already built into:

```text
getAmountOut()

↓

997 / 1000
```

Therefore `_swap()` automatically pays these fees while trading.

---

## 2. Borrowing Fee

Flash Swaps introduce something completely different.

This time we **borrowed** the starting capital.

Example:

```text
Pair

↓

Lends

100 DAI
```

The Pair now expects:

```text
100 DAI

+

Required Flash Swap Fee
```

This repayment is **our responsibility**.

That is exactly what:

```solidity
uint256 fee = ((amountIn * 3) / 997) + 1;
```

calculates.

---

# Child Example

Imagine two different people.

---

## Market

The market charges money because you traded.

Example:

```text
Trade Apples

↓

Market keeps a small fee.
```

This is exactly like the normal swap fee.

It already happens automatically.

---

## Friend

Now your friend lends you:

```text
100 Apples
```

After making profit your friend says:

> "Give me my apples back.

> And one extra apple because I lent them to you."

That extra apple is **not the market fee.**

It is the borrowing repayment.

---

# One Sentence To Remember

**The swap fee is for using the market.**

**The flash swap repayment is for borrowing someone else's money.**

Both use the Uniswap fee mechanism,

but they are paid for different reasons.

---

# Why Doesn't `_swap()` Calculate It?

Because `_swap()` doesn't know where the money came from.

It simply sees:

```text
Contract owns tokenIn.
```

The tokens may have come from:

- Bob
- Flash Swap
- Another contract

Therefore `_swap()` should only perform:

```text
Swap

↓

Swap
```

Nothing more.

Repayment logic belongs to the function that borrowed the money.

---

# What Happens If We Don't Pay The Fee?

Suppose we borrowed:

```text
100 DAI
```

After arbitrage we repay only:

```text
100 DAI
```

and ignore the fee.

Our callback finishes.

Execution now returns to:

```text
Pair.swap()
```

The Pair now performs its **final verification**.

---

# Pair Doesn't Trust Us

The Pair never assumes we repaid correctly.

Instead it recalculates everything.

It checks:

```solidity
balance0Adjusted

balance1Adjusted

require(K invariant)
```

using the final balances.

---

# What Does It Discover?

Suppose reserves were:

```text
1000 DAI

1000 WETH
```

We borrowed:

```text
100 DAI
```

After callback we return only:

```text
100 DAI
```

The Pair says:

> Every swap must pay the required fee.

It notices the final balances are too small.

The invariant check fails.

---

# Result

The Pair executes:

```text
require(...)

↓

FAIL
```

The entire transaction becomes:

```text
REVERT
```

---

# Atomic Transaction

Because Ethereum transactions are atomic,

everything is rolled back.

Even though we already:

- borrowed
- swapped
- made profit

all of it disappears.

It becomes as if nothing ever happened.

The only thing permanently lost is the gas spent.

---

# Child Example

Imagine your friend says:

> Borrow my 100 marbles.

You return:

```text
100 Marbles
```

He replies:

> No.

> We agreed on 101.

He refuses the entire deal.

Time rewinds.

You never borrowed the marbles.

You never sold them.

You never earned any profit.

Ethereum behaves exactly the same way.

---

# Biggest Realization

Our contract calculates:

```solidity
fee
```

only so that we know how much to repay.

However,

the Pair **never trusts our calculation.**

It independently verifies repayment using the reserve invariant after `uniswapV2Call()` returns.

Even if our contract completely removed:

```solidity
uint256 fee = ...
```

the Pair would still detect insufficient repayment,

fail its invariant check,

and revert the entire transaction.

The Pair is the final authority—not our arbitrage contract.
