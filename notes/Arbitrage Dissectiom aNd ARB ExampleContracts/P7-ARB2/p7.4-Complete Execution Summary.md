# Key Takeaways

- A Flash Swap lets us borrow tokens before paying.
- Pair0 is a temporary lender, not the source of profit.
- Pair1 is where the arbitrage opportunity exists.
- We borrow WETH, sell it for DAI, repay Pair0 with DAI, and keep the remaining DAI.
- `getAmountIn()` calculates the repayment requirement.
- `getAmountOut()` estimates how much DAI Pair1 will return.
- Arbitrage profitability depends on **trade size**.
- Larger trades create more slippage.
- Slippage can completely eliminate profit.
- Flash Swaps rely on Ethereum's atomic transactions—either everything succeeds or everything is reverted.

# Conclusion

ARB2 demonstrates one of the most powerful features of Uniswap V2: the Flash Swap.

Instead of requiring upfront capital, the protocol temporarily lends assets, allowing arbitrageurs to exploit price differences between decentralized exchanges within a single atomic transaction.

The contract itself is relatively small, but it relies on several important concepts working together:

- Constant Product AMMs
- Flash Swaps
- Atomic Transactions
- Cross-DEX Arbitrage
- Reserve Mathematics
- Slippage
- ABI Encoding & Callbacks

During our analysis, we also verified the strategy on a live mainnet fork. We observed that profitability depends heavily on current market conditions and trade size:

| Borrowed | Result |
|----------:|--------|
| 1 WETH | ✅ ~37 DAI profit |
| 2 WETH | ✅ ~7.6 DAI profit |
| 3 WETH | ❌ ~86 DAI loss |

This highlights an important real-world lesson: **a correct implementation does not guarantee a profitable arbitrage.** Profitability is determined by the state of the market, available liquidity, fees, and slippage at the time of execution.

Understanding ARB2 is less about memorizing code and more about understanding the interaction between AMMs, liquidity pools, pricing, and Ethereum's atomic execution model. Once these concepts are clear, the contract becomes a straightforward orchestration of those underlying mechanisms.
