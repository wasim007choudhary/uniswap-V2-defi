# `_mintFee()` --- Chapter 5: Why Can't Uniswap Simply Mint `1/6` of the Growth?

At this point in `_mintFee()`, we have reached:

```solidity
if (rootK > rootKLast) {
    uint256 numerator = totalSupply.mul(rootK.sub(rootKLast));
    uint256 denominator = rootK.mul(5).add(rootKLast);
    uint256 liquidity = numerator / denominator;
}
```

The first reaction most developers have is:

> **"Why is this formula so complicated?"**

A much simpler idea immediately comes to mind.

If the protocol is supposed to receive **1/6 of the fee-generated liquidity growth**, then why not simply compute:

```text
Liquidity Growth

↓

rootK - rootKLast

↓

Divide by 6

↓

Mint that many LP tokens
```

It sounds perfectly reasonable.

In reality, it does **not** work.

Understanding why is one of the biggest "aha!" moments in the entire `_mintFee()` implementation.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# What Is The Protocol Actually Trying To Do?

From the previous chapters we already know:

- The pool has grown.
- That growth came primarily from accumulated trading fees.
- The protocol is entitled to **1/6 of that liquidity growth**.

At first glance, that seems easy.

Suppose:

```text
Previous Liquidity

↓

4472

Current Liquidity

↓

4499
```

The measured liquidity growth is:

```text
4499 - 4472

=

27
```

Now a natural question appears.

Should the protocol receive:

- 27 LP tokens?
- 13 LP tokens?
- 4.5 LP tokens?
- 27 ÷ 6 LP tokens?

Nothing tells us.

So we need a formula.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# My First Thought

My immediate thought was:

> **"If the protocol deserves one-sixth of the liquidity growth, then why can't Uniswap simply mint one-sixth of the growth as LP tokens?"**

It feels obvious.

Unfortunately, this idea contains one hidden assumption.

It assumes that:

> **One LP token always represents one fixed unit of liquidity.**

That assumption is false.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Hidden Problem

LP tokens are **not** units of liquidity.

They are **ownership shares**.

Every LP token represents a percentage ownership of the pool.

That percentage is calculated relative to the total LP token supply.

Whenever new LP tokens are minted:

```text
Total Supply

↓

Increases
```

which immediately changes:

```text
Ownership Percentage
```

for everyone.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Another Question I Had

At this point I asked:

> **"But wait... in the end Uniswap is going to mint new LP tokens anyway. That will increase `totalSupply` and change everyone's ownership percentage regardless. So why can't it just mint `growth / 6`?"**

This is exactly where the subtlety lies.

Yes.

Minting **always** changes `totalSupply`.

That part cannot be avoided.

The important question is **how many** LP tokens should be minted.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# A Company Analogy

Imagine a company.

Initially:

```text
Company Value

↓

₹600

Outstanding Shares

↓

600
```

Therefore:

```text
1 Share

=

₹1
```

Now the company becomes more valuable.

```text
Company Value

↓

₹660
```

Suppose someone says:

> "Let's print 10 new shares."

After printing:

```text
Company Value

↓

₹660

Shares

↓

610
```

Now ask yourself:

Does each share still represent:

```text
₹1 ?
```

No.

Now:

```text
₹660

÷

610

≈

₹1.082 per share
```

So those newly printed 10 shares are **not** worth ₹10.

They represent approximately:

```text
10 × 1.082

≈

₹10.82
```

Printing new shares changed the relationship between:

- company value, and
- total shares.

LP tokens behave exactly the same way.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# LP Tokens Are Ownership Shares

The same idea applies inside Uniswap.

The pool has some measured liquidity:

```text
rootK
```

and some existing LP token supply:

```text
totalSupply
```

Every LP token represents ownership of that liquidity.

When new LP tokens are minted:

```text
totalSupply

↓

Increases
```

which changes how much liquidity each LP token represents.

This means:

> **One LP token does not always represent one unit of liquidity.**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Why "Growth ÷ 6" Doesn't Work

Suppose:

```text
Liquidity Growth

↓

60
```

The protocol deserves:

```text
60 ÷ 6

=

10 units of liquidity
```

A tempting idea would be:

```text
Mint

↓

10 LP Tokens
```

But here is the problem.

Nothing guarantees that:

```text
10 LP Tokens

=

10 Units of Liquidity
```

because minting those LP tokens has already increased:

```text
totalSupply
```

The value represented by every LP token has changed.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# The Real Problem

The protocol is **not** trying to mint an arbitrary number of LP tokens.

Instead, it has a much more precise objective.

It wants the following statement to be true:

> **After the new LP tokens have been minted and `totalSupply` has increased, the protocol should own exactly its intended share of the fee-generated liquidity growth.**

Notice the words:

> **After minting.**

That is the difficult part.

The thing we are trying to calculate changes the very percentages we are trying to preserve.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Working Backwards

Instead of asking:

> **"How many LP tokens equal one-sixth of the growth?"**

Uniswap asks a different question.

> **"How many LP tokens must be minted so that, after increasing `totalSupply`, the protocol's ownership corresponds to exactly one-sixth of the fee-generated liquidity growth?"**

This is an inverse problem.

The answer cannot be guessed.

It must be solved mathematically.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Why The Formula Looks Complicated

The famous `_mintFee()` formula is not trying to calculate:

- one-sixth of the pool,
- one-sixth of the reserves,
- one-sixth of the LP supply,
- or one-sixth of the measured liquidity.

Instead, it calculates:

> **The exact number of LP tokens that must be minted so that, after dilution caused by minting those very LP tokens, the protocol owns exactly its intended share of the fee-generated liquidity growth.**

That is why the formula appears much more complicated than simply dividing the growth by six.
