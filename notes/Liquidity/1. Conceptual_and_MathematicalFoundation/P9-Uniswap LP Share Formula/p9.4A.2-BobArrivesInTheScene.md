# 9.4 — The Inflation Attack & Why `MINIMUM_LIQUIDITY` Exists

## 9.4A.2 — Bob Arrives

# 🎯 Goal

In the previous section we proved:

- Alice donated assets.
- Alice did **not** become richer.
- She simply moved wealth from her wallet into her LP position.
- The donation only increased the value represented by each LP Token.

That immediately raises the next question.

> **If Alice didn't profit... why would an attacker ever donate assets?**

The answer appears only when another liquidity provider enters the pool.

---

# 📌 Introducing Bob

Until now there has only been one LP.

```text
Alice
```

Nothing interesting happens with only one liquidity provider.

The attack requires another participant.

Let's introduce Bob.

---

Bob looks at the Pair contract.

He has no idea what happened previously.

```text

```

(Remember that throughout this chapter we intentionally ignore arbitrage so we can focus only on LP accounting.)

He:

The Router calculates the optimal deposit.

```text

110,000 USDC
```

No excess tokens.

No incorrect ratio.

Everything is perfectly correct.

# 🤔 So Where Is The Attack?

At this point we stopped and asked ourselves:

> **Nothing broke.**

So...

**Where is the Inflation Attack?**

This is the most important question in the chapter.

---

# 🧠 The Hidden Assumption

Notice something.

Every calculation so far assumed the LP Supply was

```text
3162
```

LP Tokens.

That is a reasonably large number.

But...

What if the LP Supply were extremely small?

Suppose instead the total LP Supply were

```text
1 LP Token.
```

Would anything change?

---

# Why Would Supply Size Matter?

At first glance,

ownership percentages should still work.

Whether there are

```text
1 LP Token
```

or

```text
1,000,000 LP Tokens
```

ownership percentages can still be represented mathematically.

So why should a small supply be dangerous?

---

# 📌 Solidity Changes Everything

Earlier in this series we learned something important about Solidity.

It uses integer arithmetic.

There are no decimal values for integers.

Whenever division occurs,

the result is rounded down.

Example:

```text
10 / 3

=

3
```

not

```text
3.333333...
```

Earlier this rounding barely mattered.

Now it becomes extremely important.

---

# Example

Suppose Bob mathematically deserves

```text
0.8 LP Tokens.
```

Can Solidity mint

```text
0.8
```

No.

Instead it computes

```text
0
```

because integers cannot represent fractional LP Tokens.

---

# Compare Two Different Pools

## Pool A

Total LP Supply

```text
3162
```

Bob loses

```text
0.8 LP Tokens.
```

That is unfortunate.

But relative to the total supply,

it is a very small error.

---

## Pool B

Total LP Supply

```text
1 LP Token.
```

Bob again loses

```text
0.8 LP Tokens.
```

This time,

that rounding error represents almost the entire ownership Bob deserved.

Exactly the same rounding operation now has a dramatically larger economic impact.

---

# 💬 Your Realization

At this point you immediately recognised what was happening and said, in essence:

> **"Alice kept all of Bob's money because of the rounding problem."**

That is exactly the correct intuition.

A slightly more precise way to state it is:

> **Alice effectively keeps the ownership that Bob should have received.**

---

# Why?

Bob still deposited real assets.

Those assets entered the Pair contract.

However,

Bob never received the LP Tokens representing the ownership he deserved.

The ownership itself does not disappear.

It remains with the existing LP holders.

Since Alice currently owns every existing LP Token,

she effectively keeps that ownership.

---

# Example

Suppose before Bob joins,

ownership is

```text
Alice

↓

1 LP Token

↓

100%
```

Bob deposits enough assets that he should receive

```text
0.8 LP Tokens.
```

If fractional LP Tokens existed,

ownership would become

```text
Alice

↓

1 LP
```

```text
Bob

↓

0.8 LP
```

Everything would be fair.

---

# What Solidity Actually Does

Instead,

Solidity computes

```text
0.8

↓

0
```

Result:

```text
Alice

↓

1 LP
```

```text
Bob

↓

0 LP
```

Yet Bob's assets are already inside the pool.

Those assets are now owned according to LP ownership.

Since Alice owns every LP Token,

she effectively owns Bob's deposited assets.

---

# 🧠 Huge Realization

The problem is **not** the donation.

The problem is **not** price manipulation.

The problem is **not** Bob.

The real issue is the combination of:

```text
Tiny LP Supply

+

Very Valuable LP Tokens

+

Integer Rounding
```

When these three conditions occur together,

rounding errors become economically significant instead of negligible.

---

# Questions We Resolved

### ❓ Does Bob make a mistake?

No.

Bob follows the protocol correctly.

---

### ❓ Is Bob cheated because he deposited the wrong ratio?

No.

He deposits the correct reserve ratio.

---

### ❓ Where does Bob's missing ownership go?

It does not disappear.

It effectively remains with the existing LP holders.

---

### ❓ Why does LP Supply size matter?

Because Solidity rounds integer calculations down.

The smaller the supply,

the more economically significant every lost fraction becomes.

---

# 🚫 Common Misconceptions

### ❌ The attack happens because Bob deposits incorrectly.

False.

Bob behaves perfectly.

---

### ❌ The attack is caused by donations alone.

False.

Donations only prepare the state of the pool.

---

### ❌ Integer rounding is always insignificant.

False.

It becomes extremely important when the LP supply is very small.

---

# 🧠 Biggest Realizations

- Alice does not profit from donating alone.
- Bob does everything correctly.
- The attack appears only after another LP interacts with the pool.
- Integer rounding is harmless when LP supply is large, but dangerous when LP supply is tiny.
- Existing LP holders effectively keep the ownership represented by LP Tokens that should have been minted but were rounded down.
