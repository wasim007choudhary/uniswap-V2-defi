# 9.4A — The Inflation Attack & Why `MINIMUM_LIQUIDITY` Exists

## 9.4A.3 — The Actual Inflation Attack

---

# 🎯 Goal

In the previous sections we proved:

- Alice does not profit by donating assets.
- Bob behaves correctly.
- Integer rounding becomes dangerous when the LP supply is extremely small.

Now we'll combine all of those ideas into one complete attack.

This is the attack that `MINIMUM_LIQUIDITY` was designed to prevent.

---

# 📌 The Attacker's Goal

The attacker is **not** trying to manipulate prices.

The attacker is **not** trying to steal tokens directly.

Instead, the attacker wants to create a situation where

```text
Bob deposits assets

↓

Bob deserves LP Tokens

↓

The mint calculation rounds to zero

↓

Bob receives no LP Tokens

↓

The attacker keeps ownership of Bob's deposited assets.
```

To make that happen, the attacker needs four ingredients:

```text
1. Extremely small LP Supply

+

2. Very valuable LP Tokens

+

3. Bob deposits normally

+

4. Integer rounding
```

---

```text
100%
```

```solidity

    amount0 * totalSupply / reserve0,
```


```text

100
```


```text
0.1

↓
```

```text

0
```



# Why Doesn't This Happen On Real Uniswap?

Because real Uniswap immediately performs

```solidity
liquidity =
    Math.sqrt(amount0 * amount1)
    - MINIMUM_LIQUIDITY;
```

Using our microscopic example,

```text
√(1 × 1)

=

1
```

Therefore

```text
1 - 1000
```

is impossible.

The transaction reverts.

The pool is never created.

The attacker never obtains a dangerously tiny LP Supply.

# Questions We Resolved

Because the initial LP Supply equals

```text

```

Tiny deposits create a tiny LP Supply.

### ❓Does Bob make any mistake?

No.

### ❓Where does Bob's ownership go?

It never gets represented by LP Tokens.

Because when the total LP Supply is extremely small,

causing enormous ownership errors.
