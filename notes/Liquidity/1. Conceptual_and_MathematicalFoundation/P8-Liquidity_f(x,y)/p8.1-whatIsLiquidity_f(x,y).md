# 8. What Is Liquidity? (F(x, y))

## 🎯 Goal

Understand how to measure the liquidity of a pool that contains **two different assets**.

By the end of this chapter, we'll understand why we need a **Liquidity Function** before deriving Uniswap's LP Share Formula.

> **Important**
>
> We are **not** deriving `√(xy)` immediately.
>
> Instead, we first answer a more fundamental question:
>
> **"If a pool contains two different assets, how do we represent its liquidity using a single number?"**

---

# 📌 The Problem

Everything we learned earlier assumed a single-asset pool.

For example:

```text
USDC Pool

1,210 USDC
```

Measuring liquidity was trivial.

```text
Liquidity

=

1,210
```

No ambiguity.

The pool contains only one asset.

---

Now consider an actual Uniswap V2 pool.

```text
100 ETH

100,000 USDC
```

Immediately a question appears.

> **What is the liquidity of this pool?**

---

## First Attempt

Maybe liquidity is simply:

```text
100
```

No.

That ignores USDC.

---

Maybe liquidity is:

```text
100,000
```

No.

That ignores ETH.

---

Maybe:

```text
100 + 100,000
```

Still wrong.

ETH and USDC are different assets.

Adding them directly is meaningless.

It is like saying:

```text
10 Apples

+

5 Cars

=

15
```

The number "15" has no useful meaning.

---

# 🧠 Biggest Realization

A Uniswap pool contains **two different assets**.

Therefore:

> **Liquidity cannot be measured by simply counting tokens.**

We need a function that takes:

```text
Token X

+

Token Y
```

and returns:

```text
One Number
```

representing the liquidity of the pool.

---

# 📌 Introducing A Liquidity Function

Let's call it:

```text
F(x, y)
```

where

```text
x = Reserve of Token X

y = Reserve of Token Y
```

The function outputs:

```text
L
```

which represents liquidity.

So:

```text
F(x, y)

=

L
```

---

# 🤔 What Should F(x, y) Be?

There are infinitely many possibilities.

For example:

```text
F(x, y) = x + y
```

or

```text
F(x, y) = 2x
```

or

```text
F(x, y) = 2y
```

or

```text
F(x, y) = xy
```

or

```text
F(x, y) = √(xy)
```

Which one is correct?

At this point...

We don't know.

---

# Candidate 1 — x + y

At first glance this looks obvious.

Simply add the reserves.

```text
Liquidity

=

ETH

+

USDC
```

Seems reasonable.

Both assets contribute liquidity.

---

## ❓ Our Discussion

I asked:

> Suppose ETH doubles in price.

Yesterday:

```text
100 ETH

100,000 USDC
```

ETH price:

```text
1 ETH = 1,000 USDC
```

Economic value:

```text
100 ETH = 100,000 USDC

+

100,000 USDC

=

200,000 USDC
```

Now suppose ETH doubles.

```text
1 ETH = 2,000 USDC
```

The pool still physically contains:

```text
100 ETH

100,000 USDC
```

Nothing entered.

Nothing left.

---

### 💬 Your Question

You replied:

> **"Nope, economic value will never change."**

This led to an important clarification.

---

## Clarification

The **number of tokens** didn't change.

But the **market value** of those tokens did.

Now:

```text
100 ETH

=

200,000 USDC
```

Total pool value becomes:

```text
200,000

+

100,000

=

300,000 USDC
```

Yet:

```text
x + y
```

is still exactly the same.

Therefore:

```text
x + y
```

fails to represent the economic liquidity of the pool.

---

# ❌ Conclusion

`x + y` is not a suitable liquidity function.

Not because the arithmetic is wrong...

But because it ignores the fact that the two assets have different units and changing market values.

---

# Candidate 2 — 2x

At first this seems strange.

Why would anyone ignore Token Y?

Suppose:

```text
100 ETH

100,000 USDC
```

Why define:

```text
Liquidity

=

2 × ETH
```

It seems like we're pretending USDC doesn't exist.

---

## 💬 Our Discussion

I reminded you of something we had already learned.

If:

```text
1 ETH = 1,000 USDC
```

Then:

```text
100,000 USDC
```

can be converted into:

```text
100 ETH
```

I then asked:

> **How many ETH is 100,000 USDC worth?**

---

### ✅ Your Answer

You answered:

> **100 ETH**

Exactly.

---

Now the pool can be viewed as:

```text
100 ETH

+

100 ETH

=

200 ETH
```

Therefore:

```text
Liquidity

=

2x
```

where:

```text
x = ETH Reserve
```

---

# 🧠 Huge Realization

We are **not ignoring USDC**.

We are simply expressing the entire pool **in units of ETH**.

This is exactly like converting multiple currencies into one currency before adding them together.

---

# Real-World Analogy

Suppose you own:

```text
$100

+

€100
```

You cannot directly add them.

Instead:

```text
€100

↓

$120
```

Now:

```text
$100

+

$120

=

$220
```

Exactly the same thing is happening with:

```text
ETH

+

USDC
```

---

# Candidate 3 — 2y

The reverse also works.

Convert:

```text
100 ETH
```

into:

```text
100,000 USDC
```

Now the pool becomes:

```text
100,000 USDC

+

100,000 USDC

=

200,000 USDC
```

Therefore:

```text
Liquidity

=

2y
```

where:

```text
y = USDC Reserve
```

---

# 🧠 Another Huge Realization

Both of these are mathematically valid.

```text
2x
```

measures the pool in units of Token X.

```text
2y
```

measures the pool in units of Token Y.

Neither one is incorrect.

---

# 📌 End Of Part 1

At this point we have established:

- Why a liquidity function is necessary.
- Why `x + y` fails.
- Why `2x` works.
- Why `2y` works.
- That converting everything into a common unit is perfectly valid.
- That `2x` and `2y` are both legitimate ways of measuring liquidity.
